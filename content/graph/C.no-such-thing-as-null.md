# No Such Thing as Null

`Concept` · `geography_cartography` · *hypothesis* · visibility: **publishable**

'There is no such thing as null on a map' — only *no data*. The distinction is the whole point: 'no
data' is epistemic (I didn't look, it isn't recorded); 'null' would be ontological (nothing is there).
A map can say the first and can never say the second — 'it is never ever ever actually the lack of a
something there.' It falls straight out of closure: Earth's surface is a closed, total manifold — no
holes, no outside — so every point is occupied by definition. The format cannot represent a true void;
absence can only appear as missing information, never as a fact about the world. But reality has real
voids. So the map silently converts every ontological absence into an epistemic gap: it cannot say
'nothing is here,' only 'I have nothing on this' — and the difference between those is enormous, and
erased. (Raster NoData is the tell: a designated value standing in for absence — absence rendered as a
species of presence.) One of the falsehoods the closed sphere forces, alongside no-superposition
([[P.sliver-is-a-fact]]) and the colonial zero (the datum) — all of which dissolve once the geometry is
chosen rather than imposed.
