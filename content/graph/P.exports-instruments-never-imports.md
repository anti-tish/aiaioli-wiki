# The Field Exports Instruments and Never Imports the Question

`Pattern` · `geography_cartography` · *hypothesis* · register: **public** · reviewed: tish 2026-07-13

Geography's trade balance runs one direction. The exports — instruments, algorithms, data — leave
constantly: earth-observation methods get lifted into the rest of machine learning as a matter of
routine. The imports — the representational questions ML could send back — never clear customs.

Exhibit A is LiDAR: geography invented it for forestry, and every other industry now uses it in a
more novel way — self-driving cars, building information modeling, an entire consumer scanning
ecosystem — while the average GIS technician has never processed a point cloud. The field invents
the instrument, loses the frontier, and doesn't retain operating competence; the export includes
the expertise. Exhibit B is the missing patron: there is no Microsoft or Google footing the bill
for spatial foundation models, and the would-be patron runs extraction-shaped business at
mom-and-pop competence — all of [[C.lawful-evil]]'s appetite, none of the efficiency that lets
lawful evil launder itself.

Sibling of `P.spatial-stats-got-there-first` *(held)*, pointed the other way: the field that
*anticipated* ML's math now can't hold its own instruments, because the exceptionalism that
guards the gate ([[P.spatial-is-special-is-the-problem]]) also keeps the gatekeepers from
leaving the booth.
