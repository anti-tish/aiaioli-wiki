# The Boat Keeps Its Datum

`Pattern` · `geography_cartography` · *hypothesis* · visibility: **publishable**

The preemptive rebuttal to 'but maps must be physically accurate.' They must — for the jobs the metric
model was built for: navigation, surveying, the cadastre. Surveying will exist as long as private
property does, and a property line needs a datum. So the critique is not anti-accuracy. The error was
never that the metric model *exists*; it is that a specialized tool for one 16th-century job (galleons,
then property) became the *universal substrate* for all spatial thought — forced onto vernacular,
contested, semantic, relational space where metric precision is irrelevant or actively lying. The
argument is anti-*monoculture*, not anti-accuracy: the boat keeps its datum; the idea should not have to
inherit it. (This is [[O.coordinate-tyranny]] as a debate posture, and [[CON.paradigm-outlasted-the-map]]
as the mechanism — the seafaring purpose ended, the seafaring substrate didn't.)

And so the front door to the whole critique is *historical*, not political. 'This was built for galleons'
is a fact, not an ideology — disarming where the political claim is a knife fight in a crowded room.
Once the contingency is visible, the political reading ('whose reality counts') assembles itself in the
reader's head, uninvited and undeniable. History is the trojan horse: lead with the 1500s and the
politics walks in on its own legs. Old doesn't soften the bias — five centuries is exactly how long it
takes a choice to disappear into 'how maps work.'
