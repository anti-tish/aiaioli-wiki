title: "the rock & the map"
---

The argument starts here. Not with AI — with maps, which have been lying in structural ways for
five hundred years, and whose lies turn out to be load-bearing in the spaces AI thinks in.

The thread: every map is made from somewhere ([[A.geographic-epistemology]] — there is no view
from nowhere, and the graph treats that as an operating assumption, not a slogan). The lies are
not mistakes; they are forced by the machinery. A polygon boundary invents a fact wherever two
shapes meet ([[P.sliver-is-a-fact]]). A filled map cannot say "nothing is known here"
([[C.no-such-thing-as-null]]). And underneath both, the master weld: geography bolted all of its
mathematics to one shape, the Earth ([[C.welded-to-one-shape]]) — which is why the field that
*invented* the math of nearness never noticed its math walking off to run every embedding space
in machine learning ([[CON.tobler-bridge]]: Tobler's First Law, formally intact in 768
dimensions). The critique isn't new — cartographers indicted their own maps decades ago
([[C.critical-cartography]]) — but the indictment transfers whole to the new substrate, because
it was never about paper ([[P.same-structure-domains]]).

Then the teeth. The exceptionalism that guards the discipline is the mechanism of public spatial
illiteracy ([[P.spatial-is-special-is-the-problem]]). The gaps in the record are authored, not
natural ([[C.artificially-constructed-irrelevance]]). And the capstone claims the whole
territory: geography is broken at the physical level *and* the semantic level, same break
([[A.geography-fails-at-both]]). The cleanest single illustration — one city, two kinds of
neighbor — is [[C.nyc-map-vs-idea-space]]. The tool being built on all of this is
[[A.antipodals]].
