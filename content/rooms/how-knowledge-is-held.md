The method room. If the first room argues that the container distorts the content, this one is
the counter-proposal: hold knowledge as *relations*, and let the things be whatever the relations
say they are.

The spine is [[C.relational-ontology]] — meaning lives in the edges, not the entries. She
derived the semantic web from scratch in a note before she knew its name
([[O.intuited-triple-stores]]: noun verb noun, "holy shit holy shit"), which is this graph's
whole epistemology in miniature — the reason arrives before the vocabulary. The atomic unit is
the [[A.context-anchor]]: a pointer, not a payload — location plus why-it-mattered, because on
re-reading you never wanted the old thought verbatim, you wanted to know where you flinched. Pin
the atom tightly enough and a wide system can rest on it ([[P.tight-atom-wide-system]]).

The rest is honest bookkeeping about what gets held at all. Every knowledge system has a floor
below which things aren't worth recording, and the floor is a *decision*
([[P.resolution-is-a-notability-threshold]] — the first room's politics, running at desk scale).
Some facts matter *because* they're unresolved, and a system that can't hold an open question is
discarding the most alive material it has ([[O.ozerov-azure]]). Tools work when they're grown
from the person using them ([[P.tool-from-you-fits-you]]) — the cautionary tale being the time
automated clustering read one dog as five ([[O.539-neighborhoods]]: clustering without an editor
yields neighborhoods; a schema plus an editor yields rooms — including, yes, these rooms). The
whole apparatus points at one project: [[A.build-tish-the-brain]], a corpus that gives any future
collaborator perpetual context of who it's working with.
